## 1. COMPANY PROFILE

**Company name:** Massy Holdings Limited

**Industry / sector:** Diversified Investment Holding Company — Integrated Retail,
Gas Products (LPG/Industrial/Medical Gases), Motors & Machines (Automotive/Equipment),
Financial Services (Remittances/FX/Equipment Finance)

**Jurisdiction:** Republic of Trinidad and Tobago. Registered address: Third Floor,
Invaders Bay Tower, Off Audrey Jeffery Highway, Port-of-Spain.

**Operating markets:** Trinidad and Tobago (core), Barbados, Jamaica, Guyana,
Saint Lucia, St. Vincent and the Grenadines, Colombia, South Florida (USA).
Eight geographic locations total.

**Years in operation:** 102 years (established 1923; 101st AGM held January 2025)

**Ownership structure:** Publicly listed on Trinidad & Tobago Stock Exchange (TTSE: MASSY)
and Jamaica Stock Exchange (JSE). Free float with major institutional anchor shareholders.
Top three shareholders hold approximately 34.5% combined:
- National Insurance Board (NIB / State): ~20%
- Republic Bank Limited Trust & Asset Management: ~10%
- Unit Trust Corporation (UTC): ~4.5%
Senior executives hold share stakes; James McLetchie (CEO) holds direct equity position.

**Annual revenue (FY2025):** TT$15.83 billion (~US$2.35 billion). Revenue grew 3% year-on-year.
FY2024 revenue was TT$15.35 billion. FY2023 was TT$14.2 billion.
Three-year revenue CAGR approximately 5.5%.

**Profitability (FY2025):**
- Profit Before Tax (continuing operations): TT$1.14 billion (~US$169.3M) — up 4% YoY
- Profit After Tax (continuing operations): TT$766.3 million (~US$113.7M) — up 14% YoY
- Net Cash from Operations: TT$1.67 billion (~US$247.7M) — up 24% YoY (strongest indicator of underlying health)
- Finance costs: TT$256.8M (down 12% from TT$291.7M in FY2024 — debt actively being reduced)
- Operating profit after finance costs: TT$1.12 billion — up 15%
- Income tax expense: TT$374.7M (down 11% YoY — tax efficiency improving)

**EBITDA margin:** Approximately 7–9% at Group level (varies by portfolio; Gas Products
at ~16-18% EBITDA margin; Integrated Retail lower at ~4-6% margins typical of food retail;
Motors & Machines ~10-12%)

**Earnings per share:** TT$0.3338 per share (FY2024); FY2025 full EPS not yet published
but PAT grew 9% attributable to shareholders.

**Share price / market cap:** TTSE share price approximately TT$3.90 (September 2025,
up 5% over FY2025). JSE price approximately J$74. Market capitalisation approximately
TT$2.6 billion at current share price, implying the stock trades at roughly 3.4x PAT —
a significant discount to intrinsic value by most regional standards.

**Total return to shareholders (FY2025):** 12.18% (recovering from -24.01% in FY2024,
which was distorted by the governance crisis and leadership transition)

**Dividend:** TT$16.78 cents per share declared FY2024; quarterly dividends introduced
from Q1 FY2025. Dividend payout ratio approximately 50%.

**Headcount:** 13,000+ employees across 60+ operating companies

**Functional structure:** Investment Holding Company (IHC) model — transition from
traditional conglomerate initiated 2018, now fully operationalised. Three industry portfolios
(Integrated Retail, Gas Products, Motors & Machines) plus Financial Services line of business,
each with a designated portfolio chairperson/CEO, all reporting to Group President & CEO.

**Strategic mission (in company's own language):**
"A Force for Good; Creating Value, Transforming Life."
"A Global Force for Good, an Investment Holding Company with a Caribbean Heart."
Purpose: committed to leading the way for positive change in the region.
Vision: $25 billion in revenue by 2030, with more than 50% earned in hard currency.

**Values:** Honesty & Integrity, Responsibility, Collaboration,
Growth & Continuous Improvement, Love & Care.

**Current strategic priorities (top 5):**
1. Complete transition to fully operationalised Investment Holding Company —
   disciplined capital allocation, improved returns on invested capital (ROCI),
   Delegations of Authority refined, portfolio accountability structures embedded
2. Accelerate Guyana growth — one of the world's fastest-growing economies (real GDP
   projected to exceed 40%); new technologically-advanced warehouse in Houston, Guyana
   breaking ground; Guyana identified as key FY2025 Integrated Retail growth driver
3. Technology and digital transformation — "connected, data-enabled, people-led
   organisation"; generative AI pilot (Massy Gas Jamaica using AI for delivery
   optimisation); Orange Grove fully-automated warehouse in T&T completed FY2025
4. Sustainable hard currency earnings — currently targeting >50% of revenue in hard
   currency by 2030; IDB Invest US$150M loan secured FY2025 to optimise balance sheet
5. Board and governance modernisation — board reduction from 13 to 9 directors underway;
   ≥70% independent membership target; new executive remuneration linked to balanced scorecard

**Core competitive advantages:**
- Scale and regional diversification: 60+ operating companies across 8 territories with
  entrenched distribution, supply chain, and retail infrastructure built over a century
- Integrated model across value chain: retail, distribution, gas, autos, finance create
  cross-selling and procurement leverage unavailable to single-sector competitors
- Guyana positioning: early mover with retail infrastructure already in place as oil
  revenues drive fastest per-capita income growth in the hemisphere
- Brand and institutional relationships: century-old relationships with governments,
  regulators, suppliers, and institutional investors across the Caribbean
- Gas Products monopoly-adjacent position: LPG distribution and industrial/medical gases
  across Caribbean with high regulatory and capital barriers to entry

**Known weaknesses / vulnerabilities:**
- Colombia exposure: persistent inflation, high interest rates, weak peso creating drag
  on Motors & Machines (Colombia PBT contribution declined 13% in FY2024);
  USD-denominated costs with COP revenues creates FX mismatch
- TT$130M third-party receivables write-off in FY2024: major one-off that depressed PBT 8%;
  signals credit risk management gaps in certain operating subsidiaries
- Management depth and experience: average management tenure only 1.5 years post-
  leadership transition (SimplyWallSt analysis); entirely new C-suite creates execution risk
  at a critical IHC transformation juncture
- Motors & Machines margin compression: PBT fell 13% in FY2024 while revenue grew 8%;
  RONA flat at 11%; competitive pressure and mix shift squeezing margins
- Financial Services underperformance: PBT fell 13% in FY2024; a TT$170M revenue
  business with limited growth trajectory despite regional remittance opportunity
- Governance shadow: the Delphi Sphere / Parisot-Potter affair (December 2023) left
  residual reputational exposure and created the conditions for Warner's early departure;
  board composition skewed heavily male (11:2) despite governance modernisation rhetoric
- Share price discount to intrinsic value: total return of -24% in FY2024 signals
  sustained market scepticism that governance reforms and strategy execution are real

"Massy's balance sheet remains strong, and our ability to generate cash positions us well
to support future growth." US$150M IDB Invest loan secured in FY2025 to optimise
debt structure and support growth. Finance costs declining (down 12% in FY2025).

**Key financial metrics (FY2025/FY2024):**
- Revenue: TT$15.83B (FY2025) / TT$15.35B (FY2024) — 3% growth
- PBT (continuing ops): TT$1.14B / TT$1.10B — 4% growth
- PAT: TT$766M / TT$674M — 14% growth
- Net operating cash flow: TT$1.67B / TT$1.25B — 24% growth (major positive signal)
- Finance costs: TT$257M / TT$292M — 12% reduction
- EPS: approximately 37-38 cents (FY2025 estimate based on 9% PAT growth)
- Dividend per share: 16.78 cents (FY2024); FY2025 dividend increased 5%
- Share price: ~TT$3.90 (Sep 2025) vs TT$3.40 prior year
- Total shareholder return: 12.18% (FY2025) vs -24% (FY2024)
- RONA by portfolio: Integrated Retail 12%, Gas Products 11%, Motors & Machines 11%,
  Financial Services 15%

**Revenue by geography (FY2024, approximate):**
- Trinidad & Tobago: ~42% of PBT (dominant but declining as portfolio diversifies)
- Guyana: ~10% of PBT and growing rapidly
- Jamaica: ~10% of PBT (distribution business being divested to Acado)
- Barbados: ~11% of PBT
- Colombia + USA: ~4% PBT combined (USA recovering; Colombia challenged)
- OECS (SVG, St Lucia etc): ~8% PBT

**Capital allocation priorities:**
- Primary: Organic growth within existing markets — particularly Guyana infrastructure,
  T&T supply chain automation (Orange Grove), US market expansion post-Rowe's IGA integration
- Secondary: Disciplined M&A within existing portfolio verticals; "evaluated opportunities
  in specific new geographies" — no major acquisitions confirmed FY2025
- Shareholder returns: ~50% payout ratio maintained; quarterly dividends from Q1 FY2025
- Debt management: actively reducing finance costs; US$150M IDB facility optimising structure

**Currency exposure:**
- TT$: functional currency for most operations; peg to USD at ~6.75 provides stability
- JMD: significant exposure in Jamaica; Jamaican dollar managed depreciation trend
- COP: Colombian peso exposure in Motors & Machines; high inflation + weak peso = margin risk
- USD: hard currency target >50% by 2030; currently approximately 40-45% hard currency;
  T&T FX scarcity is a real operational constraint (USD availability tightening)
- BBD: Barbados dollar pegged to USD at 2:1
- Hard currency earnings are strategically critical — every acquisition and capital
  deployment decision is evaluated through a USD earnings lens

**Pending financial events / strategic obligations:**
- 2030 Vision: TT$25 billion revenue target requires ~7-8% CAGR from FY2025 base —
  ambitious given current 3% organic growth rate; likely requires acquisitions
- Jamaica distribution divestiture (sale to Acado): in final stages of regulatory review
  as at January 2026; proceeds and operational simplification impact to materialise FY2026
- IDB Invest US$150M loan: deployment being managed strategically; expected to fund
  Guyana expansion and technology investment
- Florida expansion: "disciplined expansion opportunities in US market" with
  "further momentum expected in FY2026" following Rowe's IGA integration
- Board reduction: from 13 to 9 directors — several retirements executed at FY2024 AGM
  (Maharaj, Jeewan, Melizan, Vaughn Martin); board now at approximately 10;
  final composition being finalised

### AGENT: JAMES MCLETCHIE
**Title / role:** President & Group Chief Executive Officer
**In role since:** October 1, 2025 (6 months as of simulation date)
**Board tenure:** Executive Director since August 2023 (as CFO); Board member 2+ years
**Age:** 51 (as of 2024)
**Nationality:** Trinidad and Tobago

**Background:** MBA (Finance & MIS) from Purdue University; ACCA qualified.
Over 30 years of international financial, M&A, transformation, and strategy experience.
Formerly part of McKinsey & Co. global M&A leadership team, leading Merger Management
and Organisation teams for UK and Irish offices. Designed and led M&A integration
programmes across 30+ cities globally. Joined Massy as Group CFO in August 2023 —
widely seen as brought in specifically to architect the IHC transition and position
the Group for the 2030 vision. Appointed CEO October 1, 2025, succeeding David Affonso.
This is his first CEO role.

**Decision-making style:** Analytically rigorous — McKinsey-trained, financially oriented,
process-driven. Will want frameworks, data, and clear accountability before committing.
Less intuitive-operator than Affonso; more structured strategist. Early public messaging
emphasises "resilience," "clarity," "maturity," and "confidence" — language of a leader
who knows he is still proving himself. Stated: "we've moved beyond the stabilisation
phase." Will want quick wins to establish authority before making bold moves.

**Risk appetite:** Moderate — high tolerance for financial complexity and M&A risk;
lower tolerance for reputational or regulatory risk given how recently the Delphi
affair and governance scrutiny resolved. Will likely be more conservative on anything
that resembles the governance failures of the Warner era.

**Primary incentives:**
- Legacy: first CEO with a McKinsey/international pedigree; wants to be the CEO who
  took Massy from Caribbean conglomerate to genuine regional IHC
- 2030 vision: TT$25B revenue target is both his mandate and his legacy marker
- Board confidence: completely new in role; Board is watching closely; must deliver
  FY2026 results to consolidate authority
- Hard currency earnings: philosophically committed to the USD earnings thesis —
  it's his intellectual framework for the IHC model

**Known biases:**
- Overly acquisition-minded: McKinsey M&A background creates bias toward inorganic
  growth as the default answer to the 2030 revenue gap; organic 3% growth will not
  get to TT$25B by 2030 without acquisitions — this creates pressure to do deals,
  even potentially suboptimal ones
- International frame of reference: may underestimate the complexity of Caribbean
  informal networks, political relationships, and regulatory dynamics that operate
  differently from the global markets he has experience in
- Fresh-eyes blind spot: only 2.5 years inside Massy; does not have the 30-year
  institutional memory of Affonso or the legacy knowledge of what has been tried
  and failed before

**Current pressure points:**
- Bridging the gap between 3% organic revenue growth and 7-8% CAGR required for 2030
  target without making a rash acquisition
- Managing a Board that is simultaneously supportive and watching closely, while
  inheriting a management team where he is not yet the natural authority figure
  for everyone (some executives were peers very recently)
- The Colombia Motors & Machines situation — declining margins, weak COP,
  no clear near-term catalyst for improvement
- Jamaica distribution divestiture regulatory process — if it stalls, it creates
  a distraction and signals execution risk on strategic portfolio rationalisation
- Demonstrating that he is more than a financial engineer — needs to show
  operational credibility with 13,000 employees in retail and distribution

**Key relationships:**
- Robert Riley (Chairman): the relationship that matters most; Riley is an experienced
  former CEO (bpTT) with strong views on governance and risk; McLetchie needs his
  confidence continuously; Riley will push on hard currency, governance discipline,
  and return on capital
- Ivette Zuniga (CFO): new appointment; McLetchie was her predecessor as CFO;
  their working relationship is untested under pressure; she has no public track record
  yet — she is a risk and a dependency simultaneously
- Ambikah Mongroo (Integrated Retail Portfolio Chair): managing 51% of PBT;
  IRP is the engine room; McLetchie's relationship here is existential —
  if Mongroo and the IRP underperform, his tenure is at risk
- Nigel Edwards (NED / UTC): UTC holds 4.5% and is a significant institutional voice;
  Edwards is also UTC's Executive Director — dual-hat creates potential influence dynamic
  where UTC's portfolio interests inform board positions
- NIB (20% shareholder): state actor with political overlay; NIB's relationship
  with management has historically been described as having "friction" under Warner;
  McLetchie inherits this relationship with no established trust

**Likely response to disruption:**
Deploy analytical frameworks, form a task force, communicate transparently to Board first.
Will not act without data. May be slower to act than the situation demands given
his process orientation. Under major stress, will likely reach for financial levers
(cost reduction, capital reallocation) before operational or cultural ones.

## 5. COMPETITIVE LANDSCAPE

**Market structure:** Oligopolistic across most segments. In T&T:
- Supermarket retail: Massy (Massy Stores) and ANSA McAl (Hi-Lo) are the dominant players;
  Pricesmart (US warehouse club) and smaller independents as secondary players
- LPG/gas: Massy Gas Products is dominant with few credible competitors
- Automotive: Massy (Nissan, MG, Caterpillar) competes with ANSA McAl (Toyota, Ford)
  and other franchise holders
- Remittances: Massy Remittance Services competes with Western Union, MoneyGram,
  and local operators

In Guyana: less entrenched — Massy is a significant player but Guyana's rapid growth
is attracting new retail entrants. ANSA McAl also present. Local Guyanese operators.

In Jamaica: retail competed by GraceKennedy, Wisynco, SuperValu. Distribution
being divested to Acado.

In Barbados: Massy is a major employer; competes with Goddard and local operators.

**Market share (approximate):**
- T&T supermarket retail: Massy ~35-40%; Hi-Lo (ANSA) ~20-25%; others fragmented
- T&T automotive: Massy and ANSA McAl collectively ~60-70% of new car market
- LPG T&T and Caribbean: Massy is dominant in most markets (near-monopoly in some)

**Basis of competition:** Scale and diversification; brand trust (100+ years);
supply chain infrastructure; exclusive distribution franchises; government relationships.
Not primarily price competition — Massy competes on availability, service, and brand.

**Recent competitive moves:**
- Massy sold Jamaica distribution to Acado (FY2025) — strategic retreat from
  a market where it was not the dominant player; frees capital for Guyana and US
- Orange Grove automated warehouse (T&T): step-change in retail supply chain
  efficiency — competitive moat vs. less automated players
- Massy Gas Jamaica: generative AI pilot for delivery optimisation — signals
  intent to use technology as competitive differentiator
- Rowe's IGA (Florida): integration completing; Massy building US foothold

**Emerging threats:**
- Digitally native retail competitors: Caribbean e-commerce is nascent but growing;
  Massy's 68 physical stores are an asset but also a liability if consumers shift online
- Fintech disruption in remittances: Massy Remittance Services faces competition
  from digital remittance platforms (Remitly, Wise); the FX/remittance business
  is under structural pressure from lower-cost digital alternatives
- EV disruption in Motors & Machines: EV adoption in Caribbean is early-stage
  but existential for a traditional automotive distributor; Massy's Nissan/MG
  franchises include some EV models but the transition timeline creates uncertainty
- Guyana new entrants: rapid Guyana growth is attracting fresh capital;
  Massy's first-mover advantage narrows as infrastructure improves and other
  regional and international players enter

## 9. SIMULATION-SPECIFIC CONTEXT (CARIBBEAN CALIBRATIONS)

**Information velocity:** T&T is a small market (1.4M people) with an intense
business media cycle and strong informal networks. Information that surfaces at
a board level leaks to the market faster than in larger jurisdictions.
The Parisot-Potter affair went viral within 48 hours — this is the T&T norm.

**Personal network overlap:** The same individuals appear across multiple boardrooms,
regulatory bodies, and state enterprises. Robert Riley sits on Republic Financial Holdings
and UWI; Nigel Edwards runs UTC and sits on TTRA. This creates both informal
information channels and structural conflicts of interest that would not exist
in a larger market.

**State shareholder dynamics:** NIB's 20% stake means every major strategic
decision has an implicit question: "How does this affect the state's view
of Massy?" Political cycles affect NIB's investment posture.

**FX constraint as operational reality:** The USD scarcity in T&T is not
a textbook foreign exchange risk — it is a real day-to-day constraint that
affects procurement, travel, international contracts, and capital deployment.
Agents should model this as a binding constraint, not a risk factor.

**Governance shadow:** The Delphi Sphere / Parisot-Potter affair is recent enough
(December 2023 – August 2024) that it still shapes institutional memory and
reputational risk assessments. Any new governance irregularity would be immediately
compared to this precedent.
