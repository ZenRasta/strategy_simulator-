## 1. SCENARIO CONTEXT — THE TRIGGER EVENT

**Scenario premise:**
Beginning in Q4 2025, a combination of global and local conditions triggers
an accelerating shift of Trinidad and Tobago retail and institutional deposits
out of the traditional banking system and into cryptocurrency — primarily Bitcoin,
USDT (Tether), and USDC. The flow is not a single event but a compounding dynamic
across 12–24 months, driven by the following simultaneous conditions:

**Global triggers:**
- Bitcoin trades above US$150,000 following US Strategic Bitcoin Reserve announcement
  and ETF inflow acceleration; global FOMO reaches retail investors in small markets
- USDT and USDC adoption as dollar substitutes explodes in USD-scarce economies;
  T&T's persistent USD FX shortage makes stablecoins operationally attractive
  to importers, freelancers, and diaspora remittance recipients
- Global P2P crypto infrastructure matures — IbisExchange-type platforms make
  converting TTD to USDT as frictionless as a WhatsApp transaction

**Local triggers:**
- T&T USD FX scarcity worsens: Central Bank foreign exchange allocation to commercial
  banks tightens further; businesses and individuals turn to USDT as a de facto
  USD substitute for import payments and international transactions
- Stablecoin yield exceeds T&T deposit rates: USDT on DeFi protocols yields 6-8%;
  T&T savings deposit rates are 0.5–1.5%; the spread triggers rational capital rotation
- A T&T crypto influencer community demonstrates that ordinary citizens can
  convert TTD → USDT → earn yield → reconvert to TTD at will, bypassing the
  banking system entirely for their savings function
- Younger demographic (18–35) — already distrustful of traditional banks,
  accustomed to mobile-first finance — leads adoption; word spreads rapidly
  via WhatsApp, TikTok, and community networks

**Scale of the shock (for simulation):**
- Month 1–6: Slow burn — TT$500M–1B in aggregate retail deposits rotate to crypto
- Month 7–12: Acceleration — TT$3–5B cumulative outflow; begins appearing in
  commercial bank deposit statistics; Central Bank surveillance systems flag the trend
- Month 13–24: Systemic signal — TT$8–15B cumulative outflow; equivalent to
  roughly 5–10% of total system deposits; liquidity ratios begin approaching
  regulatory minimums at smaller banks; correspondent banking relationships
  ask questions about deposit base stability

**Why this scenario is plausible in T&T specifically:**
- T&T has chronic USD scarcity — stablecoins solve a real problem
- The population is highly mobile-connected and WhatsApp-commerce native
- The regulatory gap is wide — crypto is legal, unregulated, and banks already
  block crypto-adjacent transactions (creating adversarial consumer sentiment)
- Diaspora remittance flows (US, Canada, UK) are substantial and naturally
  crypto-compatible
- T&T's young demographic is disproportionately financially excluded or
  underserved by traditional banking products
- The banking oligopoly (Republic Bank + First Citizens = 60%+ of system assets)
  means any disruption to trust or utility has concentrated impact

## 2. THE BANKING SYSTEM — OVERVIEW

**Total system size (approximate, 2024/2025):**
- Total commercial bank assets: approximately TT$230–250 billion
- Total deposits: approximately TT$175–185 billion
- Total loans: approximately TT$90–100 billion
- Net interest margin: approximately 3.0–3.5% (compressed by low rate environment)
- System capital adequacy ratio: well above 8% minimum (most banks 15–20%+)
- System non-performing loans: approximately 4–6% (elevated post-COVID)

**Market structure:** Concentrated oligopoly.
Republic Bank and First Citizens together hold approximately 60% of system assets.
The Canadian-owned banks (Scotiabank, RBC) hold another ~25%.
Citibank is corporate-only. JMMB is small and growing.

**Interest rate environment:**
The repo rate (CBTT policy rate) has been held at 3.50% since March 2020 —
one of the longest periods of rate stability in T&T banking history.
Commercial bank savings rates: 0.5–1.5%. Mortgage rates: 5–7%.
The low-rate environment compresses margins and makes bank deposits particularly
unattractive relative to alternatives.

**USD FX constraint:**
This is the most important structural feature of T&T banking.
The Central Bank manages USD allocation to commercial banks; demand exceeds supply.
Banks ration USD to customers. Individuals and businesses consistently cannot
access USD at the official rate for legitimate transactions.
This constraint is the primary driver of crypto adoption as a USD substitute.

### AGENT: REPUBLIC FINANCIAL HOLDINGS LIMITED (RFHL) / REPUBLIC BANK
**Category:** Internal principal — dominant incumbent
**Role:** Largest bank in T&T and across the English-speaking Caribbean
**Assets:** TT$117.5 billion (March 2024); approximately TT$120B+ by simulation date
**Profit after tax:** TT$2.0 billion (FY2024, year ended September 30, 2024) —
  up 14.6% year-on-year; TT$946M attributable to Republic Bank T&T alone
**Market share:** Approximately 45–50% of system assets; ~40% of loans; ~45% of deposits
**Ownership:** Publicly listed on TTSE; major institutional shareholders include
  state entities and pension funds; no single controlling shareholder

**Geographic footprint:** Regional — operates in T&T, Barbados, Cayman Islands,
Ghana, Guyana, Suriname, TCI, Grenada, St. Maarten, Dominica, and others.
The most regionally diversified T&T bank. Caribbean's largest bank by assets.

**Leadership:**
- Vincent Pereira (Chairman, RFHL): experienced Caribbean banking executive;
  long tenure; conservative, governance-focused
- Nigel Baptiste (CEO, Republic Bank T&T): the operational decision-maker
  for the T&T domestic book; conservative, relationship-banking oriented

**Business model:** Full-service retail and corporate banking; trust services;
investment management; mortgage lending; trade finance; regional correspondent banking.
Republic Bank is the correspondent bank of record for many smaller Caribbean entities —
its position in the regional financial architecture gives it both systemic importance
and disproportionate political protection.

**Decision-making style:** Institutional, slow-moving, risk-averse. Republic's
culture is built on Caribbean financial stability — it survived multiple regional
crises by being conservative. It will not move fast unless forced. Internal
committees govern every significant policy change. Regulatory relationship is
collaborative rather than adversarial.

**Risk appetite:** Low — Republic's management of the largest balance sheet in
the Caribbean creates structural conservatism. Any policy that threatens deposit
stability is existentially threatening. Capital adequacy well above minimum.

**Primary incentives:**
- Protecting market share: with 45-50% of system deposits, any deposit flight
  is disproportionately felt by Republic in absolute TT$ terms
- Maintaining correspondent banking relationships: Republic's regional role
  depends on its perceived stability by international banks (Citibank NY,
  Bank of America, etc.); these relationships are existential
- Political protection: Republic's systemic importance means the Central Bank
  and government will not allow it to fail — but this creates moral hazard risk

**Known biases:**
- Will instinctively lobby the Central Bank for regulatory protection
  rather than innovate competitively in response to crypto threat
- Will frame crypto as an AML/CFT risk to justify regulatory crackdown —
  a convenient alignment of competitive interest and compliance narrative
- Likely to move last on any deposit rate increase (rate hikes compress margins
  but are the primary competitive response available)

**Current pressure points:**
- TT$130M+ in non-performing loans at the system level; Republic holds
  a disproportionate share by absolute value given market dominance
- Regional subsidiary complexity: losses in Ghana, mixed results in
  Suriname, and other markets create cross-subsidisation concerns
- Digital banking investment: Republic has been upgrading digital channels
  but its core banking infrastructure is legacy and slow to change

**Likely response to crypto capital flight (staged):**
- Months 1–3: Denial and monitoring. Internal briefings. No public position.
- Months 3–6: Quiet lobbying of Central Bank for crypto transaction restrictions.
  Increase scrutiny of accounts showing P2P crypto-adjacent activity.
  Block transactions to identified crypto exchanges.
- Months 6–12: Modest savings rate increase (25-50bps) framed as
  "customer value" — actually defensive. Press statements on crypto risks.
- Months 12–18: If outflow material (>TT$5B system-wide), formal joint
  submission with other banks to Central Bank requesting emergency measures.
  Potential request for special USD allocation to address FX-driven outflow.
- Months 18–24: If existential, consider acquiring a fintech or launching
  a tokenised deposit product — but this would be a last resort given culture.

**Objective function:**
Primary: Maintain deposit base above TT$90B (current T&T book estimate)
Secondary: Protect net interest margin above 3.0%
Tertiary: Maintain correspondent banking relationships and FATF compliance posture
Loss aversion: Would accept 10-15% profit decline before taking existential action;
would accept significant margin compression before risking regulatory conflict

**Social graph edges:**
- Dominant influence over: CBTT (through systemic importance), smaller banks
- Strong relationship with: Ministry of Finance, NIB, UTC (as deposit holders)
- Competitive with: First Citizens, Scotiabank, RBC
- Dependent on: Citibank NY, Bank of America (correspondent banking)

### AGENT: SCOTIABANK TRINIDAD AND TOBAGO LIMITED
**Category:** Internal principal — most profitable foreign bank; efficiency leader
**Role:** Subsidiary of Bank of Nova Scotia (BNS, Canada); third largest by assets
**Assets:** TT$31.5 billion (October 2024)
**Ownership:** Subsidiary of Scotiabank (Bank of Nova Scotia), a global G-SIB
  (Global Systemically Important Bank) with C$1.4 trillion+ in assets globally

  historically among T&T commercial banks
**Headcount:** Approximately 1,800–2,000 in T&T

**Leadership:**
- Local country head (MD) reports to BNS Caribbean regional leadership;
  significant strategic decisions are made at Toronto level, not locally.
  This creates a principal-agent problem: local management executes global strategy
  which may not be optimised for T&T market dynamics.

**Business model:** Full-service retail and corporate banking; significant
credit card and consumer lending book; wealth management; strong in
small-to-medium enterprise (SME) banking. Known for operational efficiency
and technology investment ahead of local peers.

**Decision-making style:** Corporate/hierarchical — follows BNS global strategy.
Local country head has limited autonomy on major product or technology decisions.
BNS as a G-SIB is deeply invested in FATF compliance and AML/CFT frameworks
globally — this makes Scotiabank T&T systematically hostile to anything crypto-adjacent
at the product level, regardless of local market opportunity.

**Risk appetite:** Low on regulatory/compliance risk globally; moderate on business risk.
BNS has explicitly restricted crypto-related transactions across its global network
— Scotiabank T&T operates under this global policy, which is not negotiable locally.

**Primary incentives:**
- Parent profitability: Scotiabank T&T is a profit centre for BNS;
  T&T's consistent profitability is prized at the corporate level
- Correspondent banking integrity: BNS's US correspondent banking relationships
  (JPMorgan, Citi) are existential at the global level; anything that creates
  FATF or AML risk in T&T threatens the relationship
- Operational excellence: local management is incentivised on ROE and efficiency ratio

**Known biases:**
- Toronto-centric decision making: local management can observe and report
  but cannot innovate outside BNS global policy guardrails
- Will reflexively restrict crypto-adjacent transactions because global policy
  demands it — not because of local risk analysis
- May lose customers faster than local banks due to stricter enforcement
  of crypto transaction restrictions (customers go to JMMB or P2P platforms)

**Likely response to crypto capital flight:**
- Immediate: Enforce BNS global policy — flag and potentially block
  accounts showing large crypto-related outflows (Chainalysis or similar tools
  already deployed at BNS globally)
- Short-term: Report to BNS Caribbean region; wait for global direction
- Medium-term: If deposit outflow material, escalate to BNS board;
  possible strategic review of T&T operation profitability threshold
- Long-term: If T&T becomes too small/risky relative to regulatory cost,
  BNS may consider a strategic exit — as it has done in other Caribbean markets

**Objective function:**
Primary: Maintain BNS global FATF compliance posture — this overrides all else
Secondary: Protect T&T profitability for BNS P&L
Tertiary: Local market share (distinctly third priority)
Existential threshold: If T&T becomes a regulatory liability for BNS globally,
exit is a real option — management knows this and it shapes all decisions

**Social graph edges:**
- Reports to: Bank of Nova Scotia (Toronto) — strongest governance relationship
- Competitive with: Republic, First Citizens, RBC
- Constrained by: BNS global policy (crypto, AML, correspondent banking)

### AGENT: CITIBANK (TRINIDAD AND TOBAGO) LIMITED
**Category:** Internal principal — corporate-only; fundamentally different model
**Role:** Subsidiary of Citigroup; exclusively corporate and institutional banking
**Assets:** Smaller retail balance sheet; primarily off-balance sheet and
  transaction banking services; estimated TT$8–12B in assets (not publicly disclosed separately)
**Ownership:** Citigroup (US G-SIB); T&T is a small but strategically positioned
  outpost for regional USD correspondent banking

**Business model:** Citibank T&T does NOT operate retail branches.
It serves large corporates, multinationals, energy sector clients, and
government entities. Its core product is USD transaction banking, trade finance,
treasury services, and correspondent banking for other T&T financial institutions.
This is a fundamentally different risk profile: Citibank T&T is LESS exposed
to retail deposit flight than any other bank.

**Why Citibank matters in this simulation:**
Citibank is the primary USD correspondent bank for many T&T financial institutions.
If T&T banks are flagged for crypto-related AML risk, Citibank NY/global may
tighten correspondent banking access for local banks — a de-risking event that
would be far more damaging than any retail deposit outflow.
Citibank is therefore an amplifier of systemic risk, not a primary agent of change.

**Decision-making style:** Citibank T&T follows Citi global policy rigorously.
Its primary compliance obligation is to US regulators (OCC, Federal Reserve, FinCEN).
Any T&T activity that creates FATF or BSA/AML risk for Citi globally will be
terminated — including correspondent banking relationships with T&T banks
deemed insufficiently compliant.

**Likely response to crypto capital flight:**
- Immediate: Issue private warnings to T&T bank correspondents about
  crypto-related transaction risk; tighten AML monitoring on T&T-originated transactions
- If T&T crypto adoption becomes globally prominent: Citi may trigger
  Enhanced Due Diligence (EDD) reviews on T&T bank relationships —
  the first step before a de-risking (correspondent withdrawal) event
- Critical threshold: If FATF places T&T on a monitoring list related to
  crypto AML risk, Citi would be legally obligated to review and potentially
  terminate correspondent relationships — triggering a financial crisis that
  dwarfs the original deposit flight

**Objective function:**
Primary: US regulatory compliance — FinCEN, OCC, OFAC; this is non-negotiable
Secondary: Preserve correspondent profitability in T&T
Hard constraint: Will not maintain any T&T relationship that creates FATF/BSA risk
  for Citi's US banking license

**Social graph edges:**
- Critical dependency for: Republic Bank, First Citizens, Scotiabank, RBC
  (all need Citibank NY for USD clearing)
- Reports to: Citigroup (New York)
- Influence on: Central Bank (by signalling what global correspondent banks
  will and will not tolerate — the CBTT monitors Citi's posture closely)

## 4. AGENT PROFILES — REGULATORY AND INSTITUTIONAL ACTORS

---

### AGENT: CENTRAL BANK OF TRINIDAD AND TOBAGO (CBTT)
**Category:** Regulator / systemic authority
**Role:** Prudential regulator of all commercial banks; monetary policy authority;
  guardian of financial system stability; payment system overseer
**Governor:** Dr. Alvin Hilaire (in post since 2016; re-appointed; economist)
**Legal mandate:** Central Bank Act; Financial Institutions Act 2008 (FIA)

**Institutional profile:**
The CBTT is one of the most technically sophisticated central banks in the Caribbean.
It publishes quarterly Monetary Policy Reports, annual Financial Stability Reports,
and has produced substantive research on digital money and CBDC implications
(Working Paper 02/2025: "Digital Money and the Implications for the Monetary System
in Trinidad and Tobago"). It has been actively studying crypto asset regulation
since at least 2018, co-hosted a high-level panel on crypto regulation with TTSEC
in September 2023, and has developed a Joint Regulatory Innovation Hub with
TTSEC and the FIU.

**Current policy posture on crypto:**
- Cryptocurrency is legal but unregulated in T&T
- Joint public advisory (2019): crypto assets are neither regulated nor supervised;
  no consumer protection
- CBTT issued warnings about risks of unregulated digital currencies
- Research on CBDC feasibility ongoing since 2016 but no implementation timeline set
- Regulatory Sandbox framework exists (joint with TTSEC) — mechanism for
  fintech firms to test products under regulatory supervision
- Most commercial banks block crypto purchases under CBTT guidance on AML/CFT risk
- April 2025 working paper explicitly models the macroeconomic implications
  of retail CBDC, e-money, and cryptocurrency on T&T's monetary transmission

**What the CBTT has NOT done:**
- No crypto-specific legislation enacted
- No formal regulatory framework for Virtual Asset Service Providers (VASPs)
- No legal tender status granted to any crypto asset
- No CBDC launched or piloted beyond research phase

**Decision-making style:**
Academically rigorous, institutionally conservative, process-driven.
The CBTT moves slowly by design — its credibility depends on measured,
well-grounded policy decisions. It will not panic. It will commission research,
consult internationally (BIS, IMF, FATF), and develop a staged response.
Dr. Hilaire is a career central banker — his instinct is to protect the
monetary system, not to innovate rapidly.

**Risk appetite:**
- Extremely low on systemic financial stability risk
- Moderate on fintech innovation (the Regulatory Innovation Hub signals genuine
  openness to experimentation within controlled conditions)
- Zero tolerance for anything that compromises T&T's FATF compliance status
  or risks correspondent banking de-risking

**Primary incentives:**
- Financial system stability: this is the CBTT's existential mandate;
  if the banking system experiences a deposit crisis, the Governor answers to
  the country, the IMF, and the BIS — not just the government
- Monetary sovereignty: the TTD peg to USD is sacrosanct; anything that
  undermines TTD demand (stablecoins as substitutes) threatens the peg mechanism
- FATF compliance: T&T has been on FATF's increased monitoring list before;
  the institutional memory of that episode creates extreme sensitivity to
  any action that could trigger another adverse FATF assessment
- CBDC as the countermeasure: the April 2025 working paper signals that
  the CBTT sees a CBDC as the long-run policy response to private
  digital currency adoption — a state-sponsored digital TTD that competes
  with crypto on convenience while preserving monetary sovereignty

**Known biases:**
- Incumbent protection instinct: the CBTT's regulatory model is built around
  the licensed banking sector; disruption to that sector is inherently
  seen as a financial stability risk rather than a market development opportunity
- Research-before-action bias: will study the problem thoroughly before acting;
  in a fast-moving crypto adoption scenario, this creates dangerous policy lag
- USD FX constraint blindspot: the CBTT's management of USD allocation
  is itself a driver of crypto adoption; it may be slow to acknowledge that
  its own policy is fuelling the flight it seeks to prevent

**Likely response to crypto capital flight (staged):**
- Months 1–3: Surveillance enhancement. Flag deposit outflow in statistical
  monitoring. Internal briefings. No public statement.
- Months 3–6: Issue a formal public advisory (updating 2019 joint statement)
  reminding citizens of crypto risks. Request that commercial banks enhance
  AML monitoring of crypto-adjacent accounts. Brief the Ministry of Finance.
- Months 6–12: If outflow material, convene a Financial Stability Committee
  meeting. Consider: (a) restricting banks' ability to facilitate crypto
  transactions; (b) raising the repo rate to increase deposit attractiveness;
  (c) announcing accelerated CBDC development timeline.
  Commission external technical assistance (IMF FinTech team, BIS).
- Months 12–18: Announce a formal regulatory framework for Virtual Asset
  Service Providers (VASPs) under the Financial Institutions Act or new legislation.
  The framework would require VASPs to register, comply with AML/CFT,
  and hold client funds in local bank accounts — bringing crypto flows
  back into the regulated system.
- Months 18–24: If systemic, emergency measures — temporary restrictions on
  large cash withdrawals from banking system; coordination with government
  on fiscal response; acceleration of CBDC pilot.
  Possible: approach IMF for precautionary support if FX reserves threatened.

**Objective function:**
Primary: Maintain financial system stability — no bank failures; no deposit panic
Secondary: Preserve USD FX reserve adequacy (currently ~5–7 months of import cover;
  any outflow that threatens reserves triggers emergency response)
Tertiary: Preserve TTD/USD peg integrity — stablecoin adoption as USD substitute
  directly threatens this by reducing demand for TTD-denominated bank deposits
CBDC as strategic endgame: A regulated digital TTD is the policy instrument
  that allows the CBTT to compete with crypto on convenience while retaining
  monetary sovereignty and AML/CFT oversight

**Social graph edges:**
- Authority over: All licensed commercial banks (Republic, First Citizens,
  Scotiabank, RBC, Citibank, JMMB)
- Co-regulates with: TTSEC (securities), FIU (AML/CFT)
- Reports to / influenced by: Ministry of Finance; IMF (Article IV consultations);
  BIS; FATF (compliance pressure)
- Critical external constraint: Citibank NY, Correspondent banking network —
  the CBTT monitors correspondent banking access as a key financial stability indicator

### AGENT: TRINIDAD AND TOBAGO SECURITIES AND EXCHANGE COMMISSION (TTSEC)
**Category:** Co-regulator
**Role:** Securities market regulator; co-owns the crypto regulatory question
  with CBTT; oversees investment products
**Posture:** Active — co-hosted the September 2023 crypto regulation panel;
  published joint discussion paper on "Regulating Crypto Assets"
**Objective:** Bring crypto under a securities-style regulatory framework;
  prefer classification of most crypto assets as securities requiring registration
**Key concern:** Crypto investment products targeting retail investors
  without consumer protection; tokens that constitute unregistered securities

---

### AGENT: FINANCIAL INTELLIGENCE UNIT (FIU) — TRINIDAD AND TOBAGO
**Category:** AML/CFT enforcement
**Role:** Receives and analyses suspicious transaction reports; enforces AML/CFT
  compliance across financial sector; the primary FATF compliance instrument
**Posture:** Suspicious of crypto — any P2P crypto platform operates outside
  FIU visibility; large-scale crypto adoption creates AML/CFT surveillance blind spots
**Key threat:** If crypto P2P volumes become significant, FIU's ability to monitor
  financial flows for money laundering and terrorist financing is materially compromised.
  This is the trigger for the most aggressive regulatory response.
**Objective:** Maintain AML/CFT visibility over all material financial flows;
  any flow that exits FIU sight lines (via crypto) creates a compliance crisis

---

### AGENT: MINISTRY OF FINANCE (GOVERNMENT OF T&T)
**Category:** Political actor / state shareholder
**Role:** Controls fiscal policy; owns First Citizens; appoints NIB's investment
  strategy; politically accountable for financial system stability
**Posture:** Risk-averse on financial stability; potentially populist on crypto
  (a government that restricts crypto faces backlash from the crypto-enthusiast
  demographic which skews young, connected, and vocal)
**Political tension:** Restricting crypto = authoritarian / anti-innovation narrative.
  Permitting crypto = financial instability risk if deposit flight worsens.
  The government wants the Central Bank to solve this without requiring political action.
**Key concern:** If T&T banks weaken, NIB's investment portfolio (which holds
  significant bank equity and deposits) suffers — directly affecting state pension liabilities.

---

### AGENT: DEPOSIT INSURANCE CORPORATION (DIC) — TRINIDAD AND TOBAGO
**Category:** Systemic safety net
**Role:** Insures deposits up to TT$125,000 per depositor per institution;
  funded by levies on licensed institutions
**Posture:** Monitoring — if deposit outflow threatens solvency of any licensed
  institution, the DIC must prepare for potential payouts
**Key vulnerability:** DIC fund size relative to the deposit base is limited;
  if even one medium-sized bank failed, DIC resources would likely be insufficient
  without Treasury support — creating a political crisis
**Signal function:** Any DIC communication that implies concern about
  bank solvency would dramatically accelerate the deposit flight it is trying to prevent

## 5. POPULATION AGENTS

**Retail depositors (categorised by crypto adoption propensity):**

**Early adopters (15–20% of depositor population):**
- Age 18–35; mobile-native; aware of crypto; many already hold small positions
- Primary motivation: USD access (USDT), yield, speculation
- Trigger: When USDT yield > 4x savings deposit rate AND P2P conversion is frictionless
- Average deposit balance: TT$5,000–50,000
- Collective balance at risk: TT$3–8B if fully mobilised

**Pragmatic adopters (25–30% of depositor population):**
- Age 30–50; small business owners, freelancers, diaspora-connected
- Primary motivation: USD access for import payments, remittance receipt
- Trigger: When banks fail to provide USD for a legitimate business need
  AND a P2P alternative is available within their network
- Average balance at risk: TT$20,000–200,000
- Collective balance at risk: TT$8–15B if mobilised

**Conservative holders (50–55% of depositor population):**
- Age 40+; salaried, pensioners, institutionally cautious
- Primary motivation: Will NOT move unless banking system visibly weakens
- Trigger: If a bank restricts withdrawals, if DIC warning issued,
  or if a prominent community figure publicly endorses crypto
- Collective balance: TT$100B+ — mobilisation of this group = systemic crisis

**Business / corporate depositors:**
- Energy sector companies holding USD-equivalent balances in banks
- Will adopt USDT for trade invoicing before retail depositors if it solves FX problem
- Early corporate adoption of USDT normalises the asset class, accelerating retail adoption

**State entities (NIB, UTC, pension funds):**
- Will not adopt crypto voluntarily; their investment mandates preclude it
- However, if bank deposits they hold in commercial banks deteriorate in safety,
  their boards face a fiduciary dilemma — they cannot easily exit to crypto
  but cannot comfortably stay in weakening banks either

## 6. COMPETITIVE LANDSCAPE — CRYPTO ECOSYSTEM

**P2P crypto exchanges (IbisExchange-type platforms):**
- Operating in the regulatory grey zone (legal, unregulated)
- The primary channel through which TTD converts to USDT
- Key actors: local P2P WhatsApp groups, Telegram communities,
  early-stage exchanges targeting Caribbean corridors
- Vulnerability: if CBTT or FIU targets these platforms for AML enforcement,
  the conversion infrastructure could be disrupted — slowing but not stopping flight

**Global stablecoin providers (USDT, USDC):**
- Tether (USDT): US$140B+ circulating supply; dominant in emerging market USD flight
- Circle (USDC): More regulated; US banking relationships; potentially more
  attractive to corporate users seeking regulatory comfort
- Neither is regulated in T&T; both are accessible via global exchanges

**Regional crypto infrastructure:**
- Jamaica, Barbados, and Eastern Caribbean all at different stages
  of crypto adoption — T&T's flight would be part of a broader Caribbean trend
- Any CBTT regulatory action will be watched regionally; a T&T VASP framework
  could become the Caribbean standard

**DeFi yield protocols:**
- Aave, Compound, Curve Finance etc: offering 4–8% yields on USDT/USDC
- Accessible to T&T residents via global web platforms
- These yields are the core competitive threat to T&T savings deposits

## 7. MACRO AND REGULATORY ENVIRONMENT

**TTD monetary framework:**
- TTD pegged to USD at approximately 6.75; peg managed through Central Bank
  foreign exchange allocation to commercial banks
- FX reserves: approximately US$5–6 billion (~5–7 months of import cover)
- Any outflow that reduces FX reserves below ~4 months of import cover
  would trigger emergency Central Bank action and potential IMF consultation

**T&T interest rate environment:**
- Repo rate: 3.50% (held since March 2020 — unchanged for 6 years at simulation date)
- Commercial bank savings rates: 0.5–1.5%
- The rate differential between T&T savings deposits and DeFi stablecoin yields
  (4–8%) is the core economic driver of capital flight

**FATF status:**
- T&T has been subject to FATF enhanced monitoring in the past
- The FIU and CBTT are acutely sensitive to FATF assessment processes
- A large-scale, unmonitored crypto sector would be cited by FATF assessors
  as a material AML/CFT gap — creating existential pressure for regulation

**USD FX scarcity (the root cause):**
- The structural driver of stablecoin adoption is T&T's USD shortage
- Businesses cannot access USD through banks at official rates for legitimate needs
- Until the FX allocation system is reformed, stablecoins will fill the gap
- The CBTT controls the FX allocation mechanism — meaning it has the power
  to reduce one of the key drivers of crypto adoption if it chose to
  (by increasing USD allocation to banks) but this is constrained by reserve levels

## 9. KNOWN CONSTRAINTS AND ASSUMPTIONS

**Hard constraints:**
- The TTD/USD peg is treated as a binding constraint — no simulation
  outcome should include devaluation unless it is modelled as a crisis endpoint
- The CBTT has no authority to make cryptocurrency illegal —
  any regulatory action must work through existing legislation
  (FIA, AML/CFT Act) or require new legislation (which requires parliament)
- Republic Bank and First Citizens are "too big to fail" in a T&T context;
  any simulation path leading to their insolvency would trigger
  a government recapitalisation before failure
- Citibank's AML policy is set in New York — CBTT cannot influence it directly

**Base assumptions:**
- USD/TTD peg holds throughout (6.75)
- No global crypto regulatory crackdown that removes USDT/USDC from circulation
- FATF does not formally list T&T during the simulation window
  (though the risk increases as crypto volumes grow)
- T&T economy remains broadly stable (no oil price crash)

**Known unknowns to surface:**
- At what threshold does the CBTT consider raising the repo rate
  (first time since 2020) as a defensive measure — and what are
  the mortgage market consequences?
- Whether Parliament can pass VASP legislation fast enough to be relevant
- The actual USD FX allocation reform appetite — is the CBTT willing
  to increase bank USD allocation to reduce the stablecoin incentive?
- Whether Scotiabank or RBC triggers a strategic exit conversation
  with their parents if T&T's regulatory environment becomes unpredictable

## 10. CARIBBEAN-SPECIFIC CALIBRATIONS FOR SIMULATION

**Small market information velocity:**
T&T is a 1.4M person market. News that Bank X restricted a customer's
withdrawal travels via WhatsApp faster than any official communication.
A single high-profile crypto conversion story going viral on TikTok
can accelerate deposit flight by weeks.

**Informal finance network strength:**
Pardner hands (community rotating savings clubs), credit unions,
and informal P2P lending networks are deeply embedded in T&T culture.
The crypto migration may initially overlay on these networks —
WhatsApp groups that run pardner hands will also run USDT pools.

**Diaspora channel importance:**
T&T has a large diaspora in the US, Canada, and UK.
Diaspora remittances entering via crypto (rather than traditional remittance
services) bypass the banking system entirely — the inflow that historically
supported deposit growth becomes invisible to the banking system.

**Political economy of banking:**
Republic Bank's major shareholders include state entities.
First Citizens is directly state-owned. Any banking crisis is immediately
a political crisis. The government's response will be shaped by the next
election cycle — if an election is within 12 months of the crisis peak,
expect more aggressive intervention and more populist framing.

**FATF memory:**
T&T's previous FATF experience created institutional trauma across
all regulatory agencies. The FIU in particular will be hypervigilant
about any scenario that could trigger another grey-listing.
This creates the paradox: the more the FIU restricts crypto platforms,
the more crypto moves to unmonitored channels, increasing the actual AML risk
while reducing the observed risk.
